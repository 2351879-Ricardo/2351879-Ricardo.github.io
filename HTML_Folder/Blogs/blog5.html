Analysis of my UI & UX

While I have never been classified as “good” in the artistic view of things (this ranging from the creation of visual art, the theory, and making things that look visually appealing), it has become apparent that UI and UX are elements that touch both in the field of aesthetic and functionality.

While it is better to have components on my website that are visually appealing, the creation and effects of my current UI and UX are still very reliant on their technical aspect.

So, what exactly is happening with my UI and UX?

The Idea Behind UI & UX

The design of UI & UX is not made for the creator. As the abbreviation implies, this design aspect of any project is centralized around the consumers ("users") who will be interacting with said product.

Starting with a simple definition to work with and to investigate the UI & UX, I will be borrowing the wording by Marc Hassenzahl and Noam Tractinsky: "The term 'user experience' is associated with a variety of meanings ... " "ranging from traditional usability to beauty, hedonic, affective or experiential aspects of technology use." [1].

From this definition, it can be concluded the world of UI and UX is not something that focuses on one specific aspect of design, but rather on the appearance and functionality of the full product, how it works together, and most importantly how the user views, interprets and interacts with said product. Baring this in mind, a brief breakdown of my current website's UI and UX will follow.

My UI & UX

Clarity and Concision

Since my website is aimed to have a minimalistic feel, the need for many flashy components is unnecessary and often leads me to create only necessary elements. That is also to say that it isn’t perfect either. I like to think that the clarity and concision of my website are quite high.

From the home page, the user is met with a little greeting message with few other options available. This design is apparent across my entire website with few options of interactivity, currently limited to the nav bar which takes you exactly to where it says.

Where concision breaks however are when going to pages such as design and blogs. Since these are components that have many sub-articles and information blocks, they expand across a long area, which breaks the feeling of minimalism. To find specific blogs, users are forced to scroll through all predeceasing blogs in full without the ability to quickly switch between them. This has been noted and is in the development of being reworked into a method such that users can navigate across information areas quicker.

Interactivity

As mentioned above, the theme for my website is that of a clean, minimalistic feel. This approach however has left me only with the navigation bar as a directly interactable part of my website. This is problematic as it limits interactivity between the user and leaves users with a more static feel to the website. This is also being worked on and is linked directly to the mentioned problem above regarding the blogs.

Fitt’s Law is a law that states the time required for a user to navigate across elements on a page is directly affected by the size of the element as well as the distance from said element [2]. This use brought upon the use of larger buttons, however, seems to not have appeared on my website.

This can be seen as a direct flaw as buttons on the navigation bar are fairly small and packed quite closely together. This unintentionally allows for possible user mis-inputs as well as a general less appealing UI and UX to the website. This should be alleviated by changes in the sizes and spacing of the buttons.

Following on the topic of the navigation bar, the concept of convention can now also be brought up. The idea of convention can be seen as the “shared practices” [3] adopted by the design community of any project. The navigation bar on my website makes use of a border element with a white background to show different options on it. However, this isn’t a button but rather a text link. This breaks convention on many levels as it not only raises questions as to why it isn’t a button, but also the “button” not working as intended. This can be easily seen by hovering within the white-bordered area while not on the text and not being able to click on it.

This also directly then links to the idea of the “Norman Door” [4] and how information displayed can be misleading – referred to more casually as a bad door. In visual appearance, it would seem that this is a button, should act as a button, and should navigate like one, however, is not and may confuse users. As such, this is another primary fix that needs to be taken place.

Conclusion

Overall the website takes a nice, minimalist can concise feel as intended. It can be seen there are obvious changes that should be made regarding bettering the UI and UX (such as the long blogs page and the deceptive navigation bar). Breaking down the visual aesthetic, it is also apparent that the footer doesn’t fit well with the pages and should be either redesigned or have contained information moved to another place.
